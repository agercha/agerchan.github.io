SOLDAT

(air dry clay, acrylic paint, lighting fixtures)

Soldat is a recreation of Monument to the Soldier Liberator, a World War II monument in Kharkiv - where my family is from - celebrating the Soviet liberation of Ukraine from the Axis powers. In the background plays a song by Verka Serduchka, a Ukrainian drag queen mega pop star, famous in the 2000s throughout the Russian speaking world; who in her whole career remained political and brash in her discussion of the Soviet Union and any government she was affected by. I started working on Soldat before Russia attacked Ukraine, as a way of reconciling my interpretation of my parents’ life and heritage, the Soviet Union, and my family’s present in Ukraine.

Like many other Soviet era monuments, the one Soldat is based on is a reminder of the very real and intense pain and sacrifice experienced in Ukraine during the war, but it is also inextricably patriotic to the Soviet Union, celebrating the Red Army’s victory. They are a reminder that Ukraine is permanently linked with its Soviet past and its post Soviet neighbors. This relationship continued to change in the 2000’s, with a cultural shift from the conservatism and traditionalism of the Soviet Union. Verka Serduchka emerged as a Russian speaking pop star. Her very existence as a gender non conforming artist in this area was political, but she continued to push boundaries singing in Ukrainian while wearing traditional outfits, as well as subverting military and Stalinist imagery in her silver uniform inspired costumes. Despite this, she attained incredible success within Ukraine and outside of it, until she was banned from performing in Russia after her 2007 Eurovision performance of “Dancing Lasha Tumbai”, which sounds too much like “Russia Goodbye” to Putin’s liking.

It has been difficult for me to reconcile the difference between the country my parents left in 1999 and what it is now. My parents left the country in its infancy, as it was still building a modern national identity, and raised me in the Soviet way they were raised. I think I’ve always had a sense of belonging to a country that I would never visit and no longer existed. The idea of WWII was almost religious to us. When I visited Ukraine, monuments commemorating the war were omnipresent like gods, I read its history like a holy text, and visited burial sites like a space of silent worship. At the same time, Verka Seduchka’s music played in my home my whole life, a natural presence I never questioned and whose political nature I didn’t appreciate until much later. I began working on Soldat before the recent invasion began. I believe the monument in Kharkiv is still standing. My family is alive, but I can’t say that they are safe. I’m not sure what I can say about Ukrainians’, past and present, relationship with the country's past. I have, however, found comfort in listening to Verka Serduchka sing about times that have now passed.
